How Swahili Verbs Work

In English, we change the whole verb: "I speak" → "I spoke" → "I will speak."

In Swahili, we keep the verb stem the same and just swap a small tense marker inside the verb. Think of it like a slot machine — one slot changes, the rest stays the same.

SUBJECT PREFIX + TENSE MARKER + VERB STEM

For example, the verb -sema (to speak) becomes:
Ni + -na- + sema = Ninasema (I am speaking)

-LI-

Remote Past Tense

Use this tense for things that happened a long time ago — yesterday, last week, last year, or in the past in general.

Think of it as the English "I did / I ate / I went."

Subject Prefix + -li- + Verb Stem
Whenever you say something happened before today, use -li-.

-ME-

Recent Past / Perfect Tense

Use -me- when something just happened or happened recently, and the result is still felt now. This is like the English "I have eaten / She has arrived."

Key idea: the action is done, but it still matters right now.

Subject Prefix + -me- + Verb Stem
The difference between -li- and -me-: "Nilikula" = I ate (long ago, done). "Nimekula" = I have eaten (I'm full now!).

Use -na- for things happening right now at this moment. It's like the English "I am eating / She is running."

-HU-

Habitual Tense

The -hu- tense is special — it talks about things that happen regularly, always, or as a habit. It's like the English "I always eat rice" or "One usually speaks softly."

Important: The -hu- tense does NOT change with the subject. You don't say "Nihu-" or "Uhu-" — you just use hu- directly before the verb stem for all persons.

hu- + Verb Stem (no subject prefix needed)
Think of -hu- as saying "one always does this" or "it is customary to…" It describes routines and universal truths.
SwahiliBreakdownEnglish
Husemahu + semaOne usually speaks / (They) always speak
Hukulahu + kulaOne usually eats
Hulala mapemahu + lala + mapemaOne always sleeps early
Husoma asubuhihu + soma + asubuhiOne usually reads in the morning

-TA-

Future Tense

Use -ta- for things that will happen in the future — tomorrow, next week, or any time ahead. It's like English "I will eat / She will go."

Subject Prefix + -ta- + Verb Stem
For a very distant future, some speakers use -taka- instead of -ta-, but -ta- works perfectly for everyday speech.

Conditional Tense

Use -nge- for "would" situations — hypothetical or imagined actions. Like English "I would eat / If she came, I would be happy."

For past hypotheticals ("would have"), use -ngeli-.

Subject Prefix + -nge- + Verb Stem
"Ningekula" = I would eat (if something were possible). Often used with the word kama (if): Kama ningekuwa na pesa, ningenunua gari. (If I had money, I would buy a car.)

Kukanusha —
Negation in Swahili

How to say "I don't…", "She isn't…", "They won't…" across all persons and tenses.

The Negation Formula

NEG prefix + tense marker + verb stem
Positive: Ni + na + soma → Ninasoma (I am reading)
Negative: Si + — + soma → Sisomi (I am not reading)
⚠️ Key Rule In negation, the present tense -na- marker disappears and the verb often gains an -i ending.

Also: -ja- = "not yet" (perfect neg)
In Swahili, negation works by replacing the subject prefix with a special negative prefix. Think of it as swapping "Ni-" for "Si-", "A-" for "Ha-", etc. The rest of the sentence follows a pattern. Tense markers also change — some disappear, some become new negative-only markers!

1st Person

Mimi (I) Ni– Si–
Sisi (We) Tu– Hatu–
Ninakula → Sisomi
Tunakula → Hatukuli

2nd Person

Wewe (You) U– Hu–
Ninyi (You all) M– Ham–
Unasoma → Husomi
Mnasoma → Hamsomi

3rd Person

Yeye (He/She) A– Ha–
Wao (They) Wa– Hawa–
Anasoma → Hasomi
Wanasoma → Hawasomi

§ 1 — Foundations

How a Swahili Sentence Is Built

In English, the basic sentence order is Subject → Verb → Object (SVO). Swahili follows the same basic order, but with a big twist: all of this information is often packed inside a single verb word.

A Swahili verb word can include: who is doing the action, when it happened, who/what it was done to, and the action itself — all in one compact word.

SUBJECT PREFIX + TENSE MARKER + (OBJECT MARKER) + VERB STEM + (EXTENSION)
The parts in brackets ( ) are optional. A simple sentence only needs: Subject Prefix + Tense + Verb Stem.

Let's Break Down a Real Sentence

Take the sentence: "Ninapenda chakula" — I love food.

Ni na penda = I am loving / I love

Then chakula (food) is the object, placed after the verb — just like English!

Now compare these three sentences — notice only the tense marker changes:

01
Ninapenda chakula. I love food. (right now / generally)
Ni = I na = present penda = love chakula = food
02
Nilipenda chakula. I loved food. (in the past)
Ni = I li = past penda = love chakula = food
03
Nitapenda chakula. I will love food. (future)
Ni = I ta = future penda = love chakula = food
The verb stem never changes. Only the prefix slot before it changes. This makes Swahili very systematic once you know the rules!

English vs. Swahili — Side by Side

English Swahili Notes
I eat rice. Ninakula wali. Subject merged into verb; object (wali) follows
She reads a book. Anasoma kitabu. A = she/he; na = present; soma = read
They played football. Walicheza mpira. Wa = they; li = past; cheza = play
We will sing a song. Tutaimba wimbo. Tu = we; ta = future; imba = sing
You have arrived. Umefika. No object needed; whole sentence is one word!

§ 2 — Going Deeper

Object Markers — Talking About Someone

So far, we've placed the object (the thing/person receiving the action) after the verb. But Swahili also lets you embed the object inside the verb using an object marker.

This is like saying "I love-him" instead of "I love him."

PersonObject MarkerExampleMeaning
me-ni-AnanipendaHe/She loves me
you-ku-NinakupendaI love you
him/her-m- / -mw-NinampendaI love him/her
us-tu-AnatusaidiaHe/She helps us
you (plural)-wa-NinawaambiaI tell you all
them-wa-NinawasaidiaI help them
Both "you (plural)" and "them" use -wa-. Context and the subject prefix tell you which one is meant.
01
Ninakupenda. I love you. (the famous phrase!)
Ni = I na = present ku = you (object) penda = love
02
Ananisaidia. He/She is helping me.
A = he/she na = present ni = me (object) saidia = help
03
Tutawasaidia. We will help them.
Tu = we ta = future wa = them (object) saidia = help

§ 3 — Asking Questions

Question Words — Nini, Nani, Wapi?

Asking questions in Swahili is simpler than you might think. You often just add a question word to a normal sentence — either at the beginning or the end. There's no need to rearrange the sentence like in English ("Do you...?" "Are they...?").

Yes/No questions are formed simply by adding Je, at the start or raising your voice at the end.

Question WordSwahiliExample SentenceMeaning
What? Nini? Hii ni nini? / Unafanya nini? What is this? / What are you doing?
Who? Nani? Huyu ni nani? / Nani anakuja? Who is this? / Who is coming?
Where? Wapi? Uko wapi? / Nyumba iko wapi? Where are you? / Where is the house?
When? Lini? Utakuja lini? / Alizaliwa lini? When will you come? / When was he/she born?
Why? Kwa nini? Kwa nini unalia? Why are you crying?
How? Vipi? / Jinsi gani? Unafanyaje? / Hali yako vipi? How are you doing it? / How are you?
How many? Ngapi? Una watoto wangapi? How many children do you have?
Which? Gani? Unataka chakula gani? Which food do you want?
Yes/No? Je, ...? Je, unajua Kiswahili? Do you know Swahili?
Tip: In Swahili, question words like wapi, lini, nini usually go at the end of the sentence — the opposite of English! e.g. "Uko wapi?" = "You are where?" = "Where are you?"
Nini vs Nani: Nini asks about things. Nani asks about people. Never mix them up!

§ 5 — Putting It All Together

Building Complete Sentences

Now let's combine everything — subject, tense, object marker, verb stem, object noun, and location/time words — into full, rich sentences.

Swahili sentences can have extra information added using simple words for time (leo = today, kesho = tomorrow, jana = yesterday) and location (hapa = here, pale = there, nyumbani = at home).

Chapter 11 · Swahili Grammar

Object Infixes

How to embed "me / you / him / them" directly inside the verb.

Subject + Tense + Object Infix ← NEW + Verb Stem
§ 1 — The Big Idea

What Is an Object Infix?

You already know the verb formula: Subject + Tense + Verb.

An Object Infix is a tiny extra piece that slots in between the tense marker and the verb. It replaces or reinforces the object — the person or thing the action is being done to.

Think of it like this: instead of saying "I see him" you build "him" right into the verb → Nitamwona (I will see him).

Object Infixes are most common when referring to living things (people, animals) as the object, or when you want to emphasise the object.
eg.
Ataniona
He will see me.
A = he/she ta = future ni = me (object infix) ona = see
eg.
Ninakupenda
I love you.
Ni = I na = present ku = you (object infix) penda = love
§ 2 — People & Animals

The M-/WA- Class Infixes

For people (and animals/insects — they always follow this class), use these object infixes:

Who?Object InfixExample VerbMeaning
me-ni-A·ta·ni·saidiaHe/She will help me
you (sing.)-ku-Ni·na·ku·pendaI love you
him / her-m- / -mw-Ni·ta·mw·onaI will see him/her
us-tu-Wa·na·tu·saidiaThey are helping us
you (pl.)-wa-Ni·li·wa·ona·niI saw you all
them-wa-Ni·li·wa·onaI saw them
-m- vs -mw-: Use -mw- when the verb starts with a vowel → Nitamwona (see). Use -m- when the verb starts with a consonant → Nitamjibu (answer).
"you (pl.)" vs "them": Both use -wa-. To show "you all" specifically, add -ni to the end of the verb: Niliwaonani = I saw you all.

Ni=Ita=futuremw=him/herona=see
03

They are helping us.
Wa=theyna=presenttu=ussaidia=help
04

He/She thanked the doctor. (Object infix + noun reinforces it)

For things (not people), the object infix is the same as the agreement subject prefix for that class. The key ones to know:

§ 4 — Must-Know Rule

3 Verbs That Always Take an Object Infix

Three verbs in Swahili must always have an object infix — and it must always be from the M-/WA- class, even if the object is a thing.

ambia to tell
Alimwambia kila kitu.
He told him/her everything.
ita to call
Tumewaita jirani.
We have called the neighbours.
pa to give
Nilikupa mkate.
I gave you bread.
Memory trick: AIP — Ambia, Ita, Pa. These three always need the M-/WA- object infix.
§ 5 — Extra Rule

When There Are Two Objects

A Swahili verb can only hold one object infix. So when a sentence has two objects, here's the rule:

👤 If one object is a living thing → it gets the infix; the other object is just the noun.

📦 If both objects are things → the direct object provides the infix.

eg.
Nitamsimulia hadithi
I will tell him/her a story. (him = infix; story = plain noun)
Ni=Ita=futurem=him/hersimulia=tell a storyhadithi=story
eg.
Nilikiona kitabu mfukoni
I saw the book in the bag. (book = direct object → KI- infix)
Ni=Ili=pastki=it (book)ona=seemfukoni=in bag
Monosyllabic verbs: When using an object infix with a short verb like -la (eat), -nywa (drink), drop the infinitive ku-. e.g. Niliyanywa maji → I drank the water.
